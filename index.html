Automated and Wireless Bedroom Door Electronics, Custom 3D Printing 2020, Age 15

I'm not sure if the motive behind this project was for automation or laziness. However, I wanted to create an application that would open or close my bedroom door with the click of a button from my phone. I did this using a WEMO smart plug, an arduino, a stepper motor, some basic circuitry, and some custom 3D printing components.

Hardware Setup:
I 3D printed a mount to the bottom of my door that connected to a fixed high-torque stepper motor that was placed on the floor near the hinges of the door. The arduino was powered via a normal outlet that was always on. The WEMO smart plug was in a different outlet. By plugging an old phone charger cube into the WEMO, I lowered the amp, voltage, and converted the current of the WEMO plug output to a managable input for the arduino. I stripped a USB cable for power and ground which plugged into the old phone cube and directed into the Arduino's input pins.

Software:
In C++, I programmed the arduino so that when it recieved power from its input (the WEMO turned on), it would power the fixed stepper motor for a preset amount of time counter-clockwise (opening the door). When the WEMO was turned off and the input would no longer recieve power, the arduino would power the stepper motor clock-wise (closing the door). Using the WEMO app on my phone, I could power and unpower the WEMO plug at the click of a button. This would open or close my door from my phone as planned.

While the novelty of this was cool, it was extremely inconvenient most of the time - considering I had to open my door that way as to not damage the stepper or reverse the direction of the system. In addition to this, the door didn't open completely as the angle of motion/placement of stepper wasn't precise.

In conclusion, somewhat successful? However, despite taking it down almost immediately, I learned a lot from this project and enjoyed working through it.

Automated Filament Feeder Custom 3D Printing, Electronics 2020, Age 16

When I was in the transition of hobbyist to professional 3D printing, a lot of changes were needed. The quality and consistency of printing needed a massive leap. One of the particalar problems was the amount of force needed for the printer extruder to pull the filament. Because of the massive amount of pull force needed in the heavily frictioned system, prints were coming out poorly. To overcome this, I wanted to feed the extruder filament, not have the extruder pull in filament. I created an automated mechanism to accomplish this.

A stepper motor with bontech gears attached was used for the grabbing and feeding of the filament. I wired the stepper motor and an ultrasonic sensor to an arduino. The ultrasonic sensor was placed in the filament loading bay and checked whether the filament was slack or taut. The sensor was placed next to the side of the entrance of the filament.

When the filament was slacked and in excess, the sensor would see and register the filament, indicated it was bending. In this case, the stepper would stop, waiting for the printer to use the remaining filament. In the scenario that the sensor saw no filament, it indicated there was no bend, and therefore taut. In this case, the arduino would output to the stepper and begin feeding filament into the printer.

In conclusion, it was a small one-day printer add-on that greatly increased my 3D print quality - and automonously at that.

Designed and Built Hot-Wire Foam Slicer Woodworking, Circuitry, Custom 3D Printing 2020, Age 16

Context:
Before I learned how to cut the edges of an insert with the CNC machine, I did so manually. I typically used an orbital jigsaw to cut the 1.5 inch thick foam. But the edges weren't perfectly straight and the foam cuts weren't always the correct size for custom boxes. Since the actual process of cnc miling foam inserts isn't common, after some research, it seemed a hot-wire foam cutter was the most common method of slicing foam into straight, consistent squares. Being on a low budget, I decided to make a hot-wire foam slicer.

Using some left over plywood and 2x2s in the garage, I cut, sanded, and assembled a rectangular box with no walls. On the surface of the plywood, a short 2x2 was erected upward, with another short 2x2 above, reaching over the bed. It was like two vectors forming a right-angle if looking from the front. This shape allowed me to add a vertical strip of nichrome wire from the edge of the 2x2 to under the surface plywood. With a series of D batteries, I ran a charge through the thin nichrome wire, heating it dramatically. I added a switch within the circuit to toggle the power. After smoothing the surface, I created a straight and smooth shoulder on the right of the surface to slide the foam against. This would allow a standard cut size with straight slices.

While it cut the foam smoothly, it burnt the sides and released a lot of toxic fumes from melting the foam.

In conclusion, it was a good idea and worked pretty well but wasn't suitable for home use. Especially when I finally thought to buy a longer CNC bit and just mill the edges of an insert.
Hosted Secure Public Server on Raspberry Pi Linux, Network Administration, Cybersecurity 2020, Age 16

Context and Goal:
The long-term and broad goal of this project was to increase interest in the field of Computer Science of the middle school age within our community through the implementation of javascript in a common video game. Although generally a large field, game design draws from the fields of computer science, cybersecurity, programming, creative writing, graphic design, and engineering design. Even though it usually requires a vast amount of experience and knowledge, game design often invokes some of the most creative aspects of computer science. For a younger generation that loses interest quickly, this was a vital element of our pursuit to interest students in it. Our plan of approach was to design, modify, and create additional software for a video game, in this case, Minecraft.

My project partner and I wanted to teach middle school-aged kids how to program in javascript and create minecraft JS plugins. Then, in the backend, we would implement these JS plugins into the minecraft server for them to test and play with.

Backend Setup:
A raspberry pi first needs to be connected to a network before anything else is done. Although the raspberry pi offers wireless WLAN connectivity, we used ethernet because network speed is critical in a smooth, live minecraft server. Once the network was connected, the raspberry pi was given a static IP address. This was a requirement to be able to port forward later on and make the server publicly accessible. After a static IP address was assigned to the raspi, in the private network administration settings, we opened a port - port forwarding - that someone could go through to access the raspi from outside of our local area network (LAN). It was extremely important to make sure this open port was protected or it could have left a massive vulnerability in our home network.

On a separate note, while in the networking settings for the raspberry pi, it was necessary to open port 22; this is the port for Secure Shell (SSH). SSH is a cryptographic network protocol for operating network services to have access to remote command-line and login of another device. We used this because it was a very effective and secure method of accessing, editing, and working inside the Raspberry Pi without having to plug in a monitor, keyboard, and mouse into the raspi every time. Similarly to before, it was important to make sure that the method of accessing the SSH was protected. We used a login, private IP, and randomized 15-character password to secure the open port.

There are two forms of IPs, a local IP and a public IP. While we had a static local IP for our home network, it was important to assign a static public IP that can be used for accessing the Minecraft server and the SSH outside of the home network. This was setup in the networking administration settings with our network provider.

After most of the networking was done, we downloaded the preset files for a minecraft server from the Mojang.com website. We organized these files and then initiated them on the open port.

As of then, we had a default, vanilla minecraft server that was accessible and editable from anywhere with the proper credentials. It was joinable from any network as long as they were given the IP. This was the public, and secure IP given to anyone who wanted to join the minecraft server. Giving this IP address to players would in no way harm or risk exposing the server or our network. From this point on, the Minecraft server was up and ready to be played, joined, or edited for the addition of javascript plugins.

The focus here was to create a discord server showcasing our server, what weâ€™ve done, what weâ€™re offering, and what we want to do in the future. Attracting new players, coders, and students who are interested in shaping the server and game with their own code was our main goal. However, adding, removing, testing, and creating JS plugins is a complex process that will fully utilize all that we have put together so far and more.

Although we only did this for about two weeks before, as of November 2021, we are revisiting this project in the hopes of scaling to a dozen students or so.
